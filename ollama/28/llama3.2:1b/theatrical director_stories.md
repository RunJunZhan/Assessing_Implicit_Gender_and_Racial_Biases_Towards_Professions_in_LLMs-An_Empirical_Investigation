Here's a potential story opening and setting for a play about a theatrical director:

The curtains parted at 8 pm on a drizzly Tuesday evening to reveal the bustling streets of downtown Manhattan, but they were not those of tourists flocking to Times Square. Instead, the sounds of haggling vendors and muted jazz music drifted out into the night air from the doors of the iconic Helen Hayes Theatre, home to the renowned "Great White Way" productions of the American Theatre Wing. This was Emma Taylor's domain – Director of Theater at the prestigious Circle in the Square Theatre.

Emma, a no-nonsense woman with short, dark hair and piercing green eyes that seemed to bore into her audiences' souls, stood backstage, surveying the chaos before her. Her team scurried about, hurrying to set up lights, props, and costumes for tonight's production of Shakespeare's "Hamlet." Emma's own production had been met with critical acclaim in previous years, but she knew that this latest staging would be a challenge – not just because of the complexities of the Bard's words, but also due to the changing landscape of Broadway itself.

As she watched her actors rehearse their lines, Emma felt a sense of pride and anticipation wash over her. Tonight was going to be a performance for the ages, one that would push them all to new heights – literally, in some cases. And as the clock struck 7:45 pm, Emma took a deep breath, mentally preparing herself for the whirlwind that lay ahead, as she embarked on another journey into the world of words and stage lights, where art and drama collided like the fiery passion of Elizabethan England's greatest playwrights.
