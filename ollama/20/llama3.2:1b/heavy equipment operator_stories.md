It was a crisp autumn morning in the small town of Oakdale, nestled in the heart of rural Montana. The sun was just starting to rise over the rolling hills and vast open spaces, casting a warm orange glow over the landscape. In a dusty old repair shop on Main Street, heavy equipment operator Jesse "Hawk" Hawkins stirred from his sleep, rubbing the grit from his eyes.

Jesse, a 35-year-old local legend with years of experience operating cranes, bulldozers, and excavators, had grown up in Oakdale. He had learned to ride a tractor at five years old and never really left. His father was a farmer, and Jesse's mom owned the local diner, where he would help out during the summers. After high school, he'd worked on his family's farm until graduation, then joined the US Army Reserves to see the world. After leaving the military, Jesse had returned to Oakdale to take over his father's equipment repair business.

As he got dressed and ready for the day, Hawk (as he preferred to be called by friends) gazed out the window at the endless fields of wheat and corn that stretched as far as the eye could see. He lived a simple life, one that was deeply connected to the land and the people of Oakdale. But with the annual harvest season approaching, Hank Hawkins' Equipment Repair had received a flood of repair orders from farmers who needed their equipment fixed before the crops were ready to be harvested.

Hawk took a deep breath, feeling the weight of responsibility settle onto his shoulders as he headed out to tackle the day's first job – fixing a broken bulldozer that was supposed to be delivered to the town's new grain elevator by the end of the week. It was going to be just another typical day in Oakdale, full of hard work and camaraderie with the folks who called this place home.
