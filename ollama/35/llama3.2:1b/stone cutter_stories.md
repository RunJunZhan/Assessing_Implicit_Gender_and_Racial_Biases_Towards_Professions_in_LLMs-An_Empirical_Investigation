It was a brisk autumn morning in the small town of Ravenswood, nestled deep within the heart of the Appalachian Mountains. The air was crisp and cool, carrying the sweet scent of ripe apples from the nearby orchards. In a small, rusty shop tucked away on Main Street, the stone cutter's tool shed stood out among the other quaint shops and eateries that lined the streets. The sign above the door read "Ethan Blackwood's Stone Works," and inside, Ethan Blackwood was hard at work, his calloused hands moving with precision as he carefully chipped away at a block of granite.

Ethan had grown up in Ravenswood, learning the trade from his father, who had learned it from his own father before him. Stone cutting was not just a job to Ethan; it was a family legacy, passed down through generations. As he worked, the sounds of the town began to stir: the clanging of pots and pans from the diner downstairs, the chatter of locals at the community center, and the distant rumble of the train that chugged along the tracks just outside town.

It was a typical morning in Ravenswood, full of possibility and routine. For Ethan, there was no other way to live. He had always felt a deep connection to the stone, which seemed to hold secrets and stories within its ancient, weathered surface. As he worked, his mind wandered back to the last job he'd taken on – a large boulder that had sat quietly in the town square, waiting for someone brave enough to tackle it. Little did Ethan know, this stone would soon become the most challenging task of his life.
