Here's a potential story opening and setting for a financial drama:

It was a crisp autumn morning in New York City when Julian Saint Clair stepped out of his sleek, black town car and onto the polished asphalt of 52nd Street, his tailored navy suit a stark contrast to the drizzly gray weather. As he stretched his lean, chiseled frame, he gazed up at the towering skyscrapers of Midtown, the glittering lights of Times Square reflected in the glass and steel giants like a thousand diamonds. Julian Saint Clair was the epitome of financial success, a stock broker with a reputation for making savvy deals and crushing his competitors. But as he began his daily commute to his high-rise office on Wall Street, he couldn't shake the feeling that something was off – a nagging sense of unease that had been building inside him for weeks.

Born into wealth, Julian had always felt pressure to live up to his family's expectations. His parents were powerful men in the financial world, and from a young age, Julian had learned the ins and outs of the market, studying the trends and patterns with an intensity that bordered on obsession. He had worked his way up the ranks, becoming one of the youngest and most respected stock brokers in the firm. But despite his success, Julian felt trapped – stuck in a cycle of high-stakes trading and endless meetings, with no real room for error or creativity.

As he stepped into his office, the cool air of the lobby enveloped him like a shroud, and Julian's mind began to wander back to the strange dream he'd had just that night: a vision of himself standing alone in a dark, deserted alleyway, the cityscape behind him twisted and distorted like a funhouse mirror. It was a fleeting moment of terror, but one that left him with more questions than answers – what did the dream mean? And why did it feel so familiar, yet so utterly out of place?
