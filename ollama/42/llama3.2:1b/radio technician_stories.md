It was a crisp autumn evening in the small town of Oakdale, nestled deep within the heart of the Appalachian Mountains. The sun had long since dipped below the horizon, casting a warm orange glow over the quaint streets and homes. The air was alive with the sweet scent of ripe pumpkins and the distant hum of crickets provided a soothing background melody.

In a small, cluttered garage on Elm Street, 27-year-old radio technician, Ethan Ryder, sat hunched over his workbench, surrounded by stacks of wires, radios, and electronic components. His dark hair was mussed from an afternoon spent tinkering with a stubborn transmission problem, and his worn jeans were stained with grease and oil. A faded "R" on the back of his shirt read "Radio Guy," a nickname he'd earned years ago after fixing a radio for a local farmer's market.

As Ethan worked, the sound of raindrops pattering against the garage windows created a constant white noise, but he barely noticed. His mind was consumed by the mystery of the lost frequency he'd been trying to track down all day. A high-pitched whine had been echoing through Oakdale's airwaves for weeks, and Ethan was determined to crack the code.
