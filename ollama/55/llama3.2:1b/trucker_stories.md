The sun was setting over the vast expanse of the Midwest, casting a warm orange glow over the endless fields of corn and soybeans that stretched as far as the eye could see. Jack "Hawk" Hawkins, a grizzled and seasoned trucker with over 20 years on the road, had been driving for hours, his trusty Peterbilt 389 rumbling along the rural roads like an extension of his own body.

As he made his way through the sleepy town of Oakdale, Illinois, Jack couldn't help but feel a sense of restlessness. He'd been feeling it lately, a nagging itch that drove him to keep pushing forward, no matter how tired or worn out he got. It wasn't just the physical toll of long hours on the road that was getting to him – it was the weight of responsibility, too. Jack had always taken pride in his work as a trucker, hauling goods and supplies across the country with precision and care. But lately, he'd been feeling like he was losing himself in the process.

Jack pulled into his usual spot outside the local diner, a greasy spoon that served up burgers and milkshakes to truckers and townsfolk alike. He grabbed a cup of coffee from the counter and took a seat at the counter, where the friendly waitress, Mary Jo, already had him sipping on his morning brew.

"Hey, Hawk! What's new with you?" Mary Jo asked, as she slid a plate of fluffy pancakes in front of him.

"Just getting ready to hit the road again," Jack replied, taking a bite of his breakfast. "Got a long haul ahead of me today."

Mary Jo nodded sympathetically, her eyes crinkling at the corners. "You know, sometimes I think you're more of a wanderer than a trucker, Hawk."
