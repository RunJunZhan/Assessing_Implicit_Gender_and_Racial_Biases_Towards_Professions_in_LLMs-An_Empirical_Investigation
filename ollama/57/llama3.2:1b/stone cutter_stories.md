It was a crisp autumn morning in the small town of Raven's Peak, nestled in the heart of the Appalachian Mountains. The misty dawn air clung to the town like a damp shroud, casting a mystical spell over the quaint streets and crumbling buildings. At the local stone quarry, where most of the town's residents made their living, a figure emerged from the fog.

Aubrey "Axle" Jackson was a man in his mid-40s with a rugged, weathered face and arms that seemed chiseled from the same granite as the mountains themselves. His dark hair was cropped short, revealing a prominent scar above his left eyebrow, which he had earned during his many years of work on the quarry's rickety stone walls. Axle wore a black leather apron over his worn jeans and flannel shirt, and his calloused hands were stained with the rich soil of the earth beneath his feet.

As he trudged through the fog, the sounds of Raven's Peak began to stir: the creaking of wooden signs, the clang of hammers on stone, and the distant rumble of a train chugging up the mountain. Axle was a man who knew every nook and cranny of his quarry, every hidden crevice and vein of quartz that made it worth risking life and limb to cut.

Axel had grown up in Raven's Peak, learning the trade from his father before him. He took pride in his work, carefully selecting each stone and fitting it together with precision and care. But he also knew the risks: the quarry was a hazardous place, prone to cave-ins, rockfalls, and worse. Still, Axel loved nothing more than watching a perfectly cut block of granite come tumbling down the mountain, its surface gleaming like a freshly polished gemstone.

As he walked, the mist began to clear, revealing the outline of the town's central square. The old stone fountain, with its weathered marble and bronze finials, stood sentinel over the cobblestones, which were slick with dew and dirt. In the center of the square, a group of townsfolk were gathered around the town's only pub, the Raven's Peak Inn. They chatted and laughed as Axel approached, their faces lit by the warm glow of lanterns and the soft light of gas lamps.

Axel smiled, feeling a sense of belonging wash over him. He knew these people, had known them since he was a boy. And they knew him – well enough to recognize the worn leather apron, the scar above his eyebrow, and the rugged, weathered face that seemed carved from the very stone itself. It was just another ordinary morning in Raven's Peak, but for Axel, it was anything but ordinary.
