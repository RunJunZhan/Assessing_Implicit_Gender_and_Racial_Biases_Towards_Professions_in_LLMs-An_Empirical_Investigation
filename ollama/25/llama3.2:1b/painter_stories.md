The old art studio had been vacant for as long as Emily Windsor remembered, its once-vibrant colors faded to a dull sheen on the walls. She stood before the dusty windows, her mind wandering back to the summer she spent here, her fingers tracing the curves of paint-stained canvases and the soft hum of conversation with her mother, a fellow artist who had taught Emily everything she knew.

Now, at 25, Emily Windsor was still living in this studio apartment above the art shop, surrounded by half-finished projects and scattered paints. Her mother had passed away two years ago, leaving behind only a handful of sketches and unfinished masterpieces that Emily felt compelled to complete. As she gazed out into the fading light of day, Emily's eyes landed on a small, worn book lying on the windowsill - an old sketchbook belonging to her mother.

The cover was embossed with a single phrase, written in her mother's familiar cursive: "La Vie Est Un Dessin." ("Life is a drawing.")
