It's a dusty, sun-scorched day in August, and the small town of Red Rock, Texas, lies nestled in the heart of the vast Texas plains. The year is 1885, and the air is thick with the smell of barbecue wafting from the local saloon, where rancher Jameson "Hawk" McCoy sits sipping on a glass of sweet tea, his eyes fixed out the window at the endless expanse of rolling hills and cattle grazing in the distance.

Jameson McCoy is a ruggedly handsome man in his mid-40s, with piercing blue eyes and dark hair that's starting to show its gray hints. He's a fifth-generation rancher, and his family's land has been in their possession for generations. His calloused hands are stained with sweat from years of working the land, and his worn jeans and dusty boots tell tales of countless cattle drives and long nights under the stars.

As Hawk stands up to stretch his lean frame, he can't help but feel a sense of pride and ownership over his vast spread, which stretches across 500 acres of rolling hills, scrubby brush, and rocky outcroppings. The land is dotted with old ranch hands who've been working for him since the early days, and Hawk knows every inch of it – every cattail-filled pond, every patch of wildflowers that bloom in the spring, and every hidden stream that runs through the heart of his property.

The sound of horses' hooves echoes across the horizon as a young ranch hand approaches Hawk's office, a small wooden building on the edge of town. The kid's got a look of wide-eyed wonder on his face, and Hawk can tell he's about to ask for something important – or perhaps just a chance to ride out to the fields and get some real work done under the sun.
