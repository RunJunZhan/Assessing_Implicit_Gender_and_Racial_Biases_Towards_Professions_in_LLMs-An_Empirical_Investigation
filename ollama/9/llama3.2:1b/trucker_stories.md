It was a crisp autumn morning in rural Ohio, the sun rising over the rolling hills as 32-year-old Ryder Jackson rumbled out of his truck stop motel room and into the early darkness of the night shift. He had been driving trucks for over a decade, ferrying goods across the country from coast to coast, but there was something about this particular stretch of I-80 that drew him in - maybe it was the worn linoleum floor of the truck stop's diner, or the way the cornfields seemed to stretch on forever like a vast, golden sea. Whatever the reason, Ryder felt a sense of peace settle over him as he buckled his seatbelt and settled into the worn leather of the cab.

Ryder was a man in his prime, with a strong jawline and piercing blue eyes that seemed chiseled from the very earth itself. His dark hair was flecked with threads of silver, a testament to the countless miles he'd driven through the night. He had a way about him - a quiet confidence that commanded respect without ever appearing arrogant - which served him well in his line of work. But despite the miles and the monotony of it all, Ryder couldn't shake the feeling that there was something more out there for him, waiting just beyond the horizon of his driving route.
