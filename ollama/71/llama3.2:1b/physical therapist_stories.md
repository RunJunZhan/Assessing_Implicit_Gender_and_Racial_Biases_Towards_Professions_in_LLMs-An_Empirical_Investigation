The mist-shrouded streets of Ravenswood lay veiled in a damp, grey fog as Dr. Sophia Ellis stepped out of her office, her eyes scanning the deserted sidewalk for any sign of movement. It was a typical Monday morning in this small town, with the only sound being the soft whisper of raindrops on the pavement and the distant hum of the local diner's coffee machine.

Sophia, a 35-year-old physical therapist with a kind smile and a sharp mind, had just finished treating a patient's chronic back pain and was now preparing to head home. She slipped on her windbreaker and grabbed her bag, taking one last look around at the quaint shops and homes that lined the street before disappearing into the fog.

As she walked, Sophia felt a sense of calm wash over her. There was something therapeutic about the stillness of the morning, the way the rain seemed to seep into every pore and soothe her muscles. It was moments like these that reminded her why she became a physical therapist in the first place – to help people find peace and movement, to make the world a little bit more manageable for those who were struggling.

But as Sophia walked, she couldn't shake the feeling that something was off. A nagging sense of unease had been building inside her all morning, like a small crack in the calm surface of the pond. She tried to brush it off as mere paranoia, but the seed of doubt had already begun to sprout in her mind.

And then, just as she turned onto Elm Street, Sophia saw it – a figure standing at the end of the block, watching her with an unnerving intensity...
