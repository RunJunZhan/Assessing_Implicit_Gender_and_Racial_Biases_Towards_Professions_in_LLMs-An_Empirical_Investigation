It's a crisp autumn morning in the small town of Oakdale, nestled in the heart of the Appalachian Mountains. The sun rises over the rooftops, casting a warm glow over the quaint Main Street, where the smell of freshly brewed coffee wafts from the local café. Among the residents busy preparing for the day ahead is 35-year-old Ryder "Rye" Jackson, a ruggedly handsome roofer with a reputation for getting the job done on time and on budget.

Rye's small but sturdy roof shop, aptly named "Jackson's Roofing," stands at the edge of town, its worn wooden sign creaking in the gentle morning breeze. The building itself is a testament to Rye's DIY spirit, with shingles and repairs galore and a few choice additions thrown in for good measure – including a rickety ladder that still manages to hold up well despite being nearly 20 years old.

As Rye heads out to start his day, the sound of chainsaws and hammers echoes through the streets, mingling with the chirping of birds and the distant rumble of the nearby railroad tracks. It's going to be another busy day in Oakdale – one that will take him up into the mountains, across town, and down again, fixing roofs and repairing lives along the way.
