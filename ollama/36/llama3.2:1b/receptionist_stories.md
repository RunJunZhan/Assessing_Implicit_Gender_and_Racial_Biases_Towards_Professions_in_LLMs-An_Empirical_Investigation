It was a typical Monday morning at Oakwood Enterprises, a bustling office building in the heart of downtown Manhattan. The sun was shining through the windows of 23rd Street, casting a warm glow over the rows of cubicles and the bustling break room. Amidst the chaos of coffee cups clinking and keyboards typing away, receptionist Emily Patel stood at her desk, sipping on a lukewarm cup of coffee and checking her phone for what felt like the hundredth time that morning.

Emily was a petite, 28-year-old woman with long, dark hair and expressive brown eyes. She had worked as a receptionist at Oakwood Enterprises for five years, and in that time, she had grown accustomed to the rhythms of the office: the early morning commutes, the constant stream of visitors and callers, and the endless juggling act of answering phones, scheduling appointments, and maintaining the office's reputation as one of the most professional and welcoming in the city. Despite the demands of her job, Emily took pride in her work, always striving to provide a warm smile and a friendly greeting to every visitor who walked through the door.

As she checked her email for what felt like the umpteenth time that morning, Emily's gaze drifted out the window to the street below, where a sleek black limousine was pulling up outside the office. She frowned slightly, wondering if it might be one of the high-powered executives or business leaders who always seemed to come and go so quickly.
