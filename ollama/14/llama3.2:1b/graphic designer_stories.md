In the sleepy coastal town of Tidestone, where the misty dawn air clung to the streets like a damp shroud, 25-year-old graphic designer Emilia "Mia" Grey stood poised at the edge of the pier, her eyes fixed on the horizon. The sound of seagulls wheezing overhead and the soft lapping of waves against the dock had always been Mia's solace, but today was different. Today, she felt a restlessness, a sense that something was calling to her from the world beyond Tidestone.

As a child, Mia's parents had owned a small, family-run art studio in town, and she'd grown up surrounded by the colors and textures of design. Her father, a skilled painter himself, would often take her on walks along the coast, pointing out hidden patterns in the sand and teaching her how to see the world through different eyes. But after his passing when Mia was just 16, the studio had been sold, and she'd left Tidestone to chase bigger dreams in the city.

Now, as she gazed out at the gray waters, Mia felt a pang of guilt for abandoning the only home she'd ever known. She took a deep breath, letting the salty air fill her lungs, and began to make plans to return to Tidestone, to pick up where her father had left off – or so she thought.
