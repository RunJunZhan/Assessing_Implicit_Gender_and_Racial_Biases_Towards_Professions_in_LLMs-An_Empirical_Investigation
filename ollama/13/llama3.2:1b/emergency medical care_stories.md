It was a chilly autumn evening in the small town of Oakdale, where the streets were lined with towering oak trees and the houses seemed to lean in, as if sharing secrets. The air was crisp and clean, carrying the sweet scent of ripe hickories and the distant hint of wood smoke.

In the midst of this serene landscape, a lone figure emerged from the shadows. Dr. Emily Thompson, a 35-year-old emergency medical technician (EMT), stepped out into the night, her dark hair pulled back into a tight ponytail, and her eyes scanning the darkness for any sign of distress. She was headed to the Oakdale Medical Center, her home away from home.

As she walked down the main street, the flickering neon signs of the local shops and restaurants cast colorful shadows on the pavement, making it seem as though the town itself was alive and watching her. The streets were quiet, save for the occasional crunch of leaves beneath Emily's feet or the distant hum of a passing car.

Emily had grown up in Oakdale, and despite its small size, she knew every nook and cranny, every hidden alleyway and every shortcut. She had trained to be an EMT since her early twenties, and over the years, she had seen it all – from the worst accidents to the most routine injuries.

As she turned onto her street, Emily noticed a faint glow emanating from the old oak tree in front of 142 Oak Street. She quickened her pace, her heart beating just a little bit faster with anticipation. Something was off.
