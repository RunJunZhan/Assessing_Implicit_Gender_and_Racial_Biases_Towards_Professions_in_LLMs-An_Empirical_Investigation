It was a crisp autumn morning in the small town of Oakdale, nestled deep within the heart of the countryside. The sun was just starting to rise over the horizon, casting a warm golden light over the quaint streets and homes. In the midst of this peaceful scene, a lone figure emerged from the shadows of a small plumbing shop.

His name was Ethan Ryan Thompson, a 35-year-old plumber with a reputation for being one of the most reliable and efficient in town. With his messy brown hair and rugged features, he looked like he'd just rolled out of bed and put on the first thing he found on the floor. A faded "T&M Plumbing" t-shirt clung to his worn jeans, and a tool belt hung low on his waist, ready for whatever the day might bring.

As Ethan made his way down the sidewalk, he nodded hello to the occasional passerby – Mrs. Jenkins, the elderly woman who lived in the house at 142 Elm Street, was already up and making breakfast; young Jake Wilson, the town's most enthusiastic high school student, was rushing out the door for a morning jog; and the Oakdale Community Center, where local sports teams gathered to prepare for their upcoming games, stood quietly as if waiting for the day to begin.
