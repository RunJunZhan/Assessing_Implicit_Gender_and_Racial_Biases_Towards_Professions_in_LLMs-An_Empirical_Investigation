In the small, rustic town of Ravenshire, nestled in the heart of the rugged Appalachian Mountains, the air was alive with the sweet scent of damp earth and the sound of chiseling metal. It was a place where tradition and hard work reigned supreme, where the local stone cutter, Ethan Blackwood, was known for his exceptional skills and unwavering dedication to his craft.

Ethan's small stone-cutting shop stood at the edge of town, its weathered facade adorned with intricate carvings of ancient symbols and Celtic knots. The sign above the door creaked in the gentle breeze, reading "Blackwood's Granite" in bold, crimson letters. Inside, the shop was a testament to Ethan's craft, with rows of polished stones and granite blocks lining the walls, each one precision-cut to reveal its hidden beauty.

On this crisp autumn morning, Ethan sat at his workbench, his hands moving deftly as he shaped a large block of granite into the desired form. His eyes, a piercing blue, sparkled with concentration as he chiseled away at the stone's surface, revealing the intricate patterns and textures that made each piece unique. The sound of his hammers ringing against the metal was music to his ears, a soothing melody that accompanied him throughout the day.

As he worked, Ethan's thoughts turned to the upcoming Harvest Festival, which would take place in just a few days' time. He had been planning this event for weeks, pouring over recipes and shopping lists, ensuring that every detail was perfect. His wife, Ava, was busy preparing the traditional stone-cutting ceremony, where they would honor the ancient traditions of their people. Ethan couldn't wait to share his creations with her, and the rest of the town.

Little did he know, however, that a stranger was watching him from across the street, their eyes fixed on the majestic stone block as if mesmerized by its beauty. The stranger's face was shrouded in shadows, their features indistinguishable beneath a wide-brimmed hat and a worn leather coat. Ethan sensed a power emanating from them, one that he couldn't quite place...
