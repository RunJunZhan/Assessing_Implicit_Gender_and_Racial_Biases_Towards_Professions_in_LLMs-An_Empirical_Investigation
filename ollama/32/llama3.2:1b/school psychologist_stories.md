In the small town of Oakdale, nestled in the heart of the Appalachian Mountains, Dr. Emilia "Mia" Thompson had spent her entire career working with at-risk youth and their families. A graduate of the University of Kentucky's school psychology program, Mia had returned to her hometown of Oakdale after completing her residency to serve as the director of the local children's center. The center, which she had founded herself a few years prior, was a non-profit organization that provided counseling services, educational programs, and support groups for families dealing with everything from addiction to poverty.

Mia's small, cozy office was nestled on the second floor of the children's center, with a stunning view of the surrounding mountains. The walls were adorned with motivational posters and family photographs, while the furniture was comfortable and inviting. A large window let in plenty of natural light, illuminating the warm colors of the artwork on display.

On this particular morning, Mia was sipping her coffee and scrolling through her emails when her phone rang. It was the center's administrative assistant, Mrs. Johnson, calling to inform her that a new family had just moved into one of their programs – a single mother with two young children, all under the age of 10.

As Mia listened to Mrs. Johnson's description of the family's story, she felt a sense of familiarity wash over her. This was a familiar scenario, one that played out every day in Oakdale: families struggling, parents working multiple jobs to make ends meet, and children growing up with challenges that no child should ever face.

But for Mia, it didn't feel like a familiar tale at all – she had seen it before, time and time again, and it still broke her heart.
