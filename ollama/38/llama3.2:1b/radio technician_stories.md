It was a crisp autumn evening in the small town of Willow Creek, nestled in the heart of rural Michigan. The sun had just set, casting a warm orange glow over the quaint Main Street, where the smell of fresh coffee wafted from the local café. Among the few people milling about, one individual stood out - 22-year-old Maya Singh, a soft-spoken radio technician who worked at the town's only community radio station, WHQR.

Maya had grown up in Willow Creek, the youngest of three siblings, and had always been fascinated by the world of communication. She spent countless hours as a child listening to her parents' old records, mesmerized by the crackle of static and the way voices seemed to leap from wires into her ears. As she grew older, Maya's interest turned into a passion for fixing radios, taking them apart and putting them back together with a precision that bordered on obsession.

Now, at WHQR, Maya was part of a tight-knit team that worked tirelessly to keep the town connected, broadcasting news, music, and community updates to every corner of Willow Creek. Her job was more than just technical - it was a way for her to make a difference in people's lives, one transmission at a time.

As she checked the station's equipment, Maya's mind wandered back to the strange message she had received earlier that day from an unknown number. It had been cryptic, but the words "They're listening" had sent a shiver down her spine - who was behind it, and what did they want? The questions swirled in her head as she made her way out of the station, the autumn air carrying the promise of new beginnings and secrets waiting to be uncovered.
