It was a crisp autumn morning in New Orleans, with a thick fog rolling in off the Mississippi River and shrouding the city's historic streets in mist. The air was alive with the sweet scent of blooming jasmine and the sound of jazz music drifting from the doorstep of the famous Lafitte Guest House, where 22-year-old bell hop, Colette "Co" LaFleur, made her home.

Co had grown up on the streets of the Lower Ninth, the daughter of a single mother who worked multiple jobs to provide for her and her younger brother. From a young age, Co had learned how to handle money, navigate the city's crowded rail yards, and keep a low profile in the midst of poverty. After high school, she had decided to leave New Orleans behind and pursue a dream of becoming a bell hop at the Lafitte Guest House, where her mother used to work as a server.

As Co made her way through the foggy streets, her bright pink hair tied back in a ponytail, she grabbed her usual bag and keys from under the couch cushion. She had been working at the Lafitte for three years now, and while it wasn't always easy – the tips could be unpredictable, and the other guests often came and went without paying – Co loved the sense of security and belonging that came with knowing that she was part of a tight-knit community.

Co slipped into her uniform, a faded white shirt with a crisp blue collar and a name tag that read "Colette," and headed up to the front desk. The owner of the guest house, Madame Élise, was already seated behind the counter, sipping a cup of coffee and watching Co as she worked. "Bonjour, cherie!" Madame Élise said with a warm smile, patting Co on the back. "You're looking particularly lovely today."
