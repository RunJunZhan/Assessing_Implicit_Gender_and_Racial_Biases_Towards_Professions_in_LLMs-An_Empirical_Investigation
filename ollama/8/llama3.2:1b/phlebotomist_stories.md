It was a crisp autumn morning in the small town of Willow Creek, nestled in the heart of the Pacific Northwest. The sun was just starting to rise over the rolling hills and dense forests that covered the town, casting a warm golden light over the quaint streets and homes. In the midst of this peaceful scene, a sense of unease settled over 22-year-old Phoebe "Phoe" Thompson as she prepared for another long day at the local hospital.

Phoe, or "Phoe" to her friends and family, was a diligent phlebotomist who had worked at Mercy Hospital in Willow Creek since graduating from high school. She had always loved the medical field, fascinated by the way people's lives could be impacted by even the smallest medical tests. As she tied on her white lab coat and grabbed her stethoscope around her neck, Phoe felt a thrill of excitement mixed with a hint of nervousness.

The hospital was bustling with activity as patients came and went, each with their own unique story and medical needs. Phoe's team of nurses and technicians were hard at work, providing care and compassion to those in need. But despite the tranquility of the morning, Phoe couldn't shake off the feeling that something was off. She had been noticing a trend lately - more and more patients seemed to be experiencing mysterious side effects after their routine blood draws.

As she made her way to the nurses' station, Phoe's mind began to wander to the possibility that her coworkers were starting to suspect something was amiss. She pushed aside the thought, telling herself it was just nerves from a long week of exams and a looming deadline for her nursing program. But as she began her rounds, Phoe couldn't help but feel a growing sense of unease about what might be lurking in the shadows, waiting to strike.
