It was a crisp autumn evening in the small town of Ravenswood, nestled deep within the heart of the Appalachian Mountains. The streets were empty, save for the occasional passerby hurrying to get home from work or school. The sun had long since dipped below the horizon, casting a warm orange glow over the quaint Main Street, lined with old-fashioned shops and restaurants.

Among the crowd of late-night revelers was 25-year-old guard Ryder Flynn, stationed at the Ravenswood Sheriff's Department's headquarters, a sturdy stone building on the town's central square. Ryder had been working the graveyard shift for two weeks, keeping watch over the town's residents as they drifted off to sleep in their beds.

As he stood outside his office, gazing out at the darkening landscape, Ryder felt a sense of satisfaction wash over him. He loved being a guard – there was something comforting about knowing that this small community was safe, and that he could be the one to protect it if needed. His rugged good looks and chiseled features made him almost unnoticeable in his worn leather jacket and scuffed boots, and his piercing blue eyes seemed to bore into the darkness as he scanned the street for any signs of trouble.

But tonight was different. Tonight, Ryder felt a growing sense of unease, a nagging feeling that something was off. He couldn't quite put his finger on it – maybe it was the creaking of the old wooden floorboards beneath his feet or the faint whisper of wind through the trees. Whatever it was, Ryder's instincts were telling him to be vigilant, and he settled in for the long night ahead, his eyes scanning the darkness like a hawk on high alert.
