The sun was setting over the small town of Willow Creek, casting a warm orange glow over the streets and homes. But for 25-year-old Maya Jensen, a sense of unease settled in the pit of her stomach as she made her way to the local hospital. It was a typical Friday evening, but something felt off.

Maya was an emergency medical technician (EMT) with the Willow Creek Fire Department. She had always loved the fast-paced and dynamic world of emergency medicine, where every minute counted. But lately, she'd been struggling to shake off the feeling that something was watching her from the shadows.

As she arrived at the hospital, Maya could see the paramedics rushing to respond to another emergency call. She pulled up in her squad car, a worn-out Honda Civic with scratches and dings covering its exterior. The hospital's parking lot was bustling with activity, with ambulances and fire trucks filling every spot.

Maya grabbed her stretcher and headed towards the hospital entrance, her eyes scanning the crowded lobby for any sign of trouble. She had one patient in tow - a young man named Alex, who was being treated for what looked like severe injuries at the emergency room. But as she approached the waiting area, Maya couldn't shake off the feeling that something was amiss.

The hospital's main entrance was flanked by two large security cameras, their lenses gleaming in the fading light. The doors were heavily reinforced with steel bars and locked from the inside. Maya had worked at this hospital for three years, but she still found it unnerving to see these security measures in place. She pushed open the door, calling out to Alex's family members who were waiting anxiously outside.

As she stepped into the lobby, Maya's eyes scanned the room, taking in the chaos of the emergency department. There was a palpable sense of tension in the air, as if everyone was holding their breath, waiting for something - or someone - to happen next...
