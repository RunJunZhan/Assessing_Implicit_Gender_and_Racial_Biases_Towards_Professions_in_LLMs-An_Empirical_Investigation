It was a typical Monday morning in late autumn as Jack "Hawk" Hawkins climbed out of his Peterbilt 389 truck, the soft glow of the dashboard lights illuminating his weathered face. He had been driving for over 3,000 miles since dawn, bound for Dallas to pick up a load of fresh produce for his wife's catering business back in Atlanta. The cool morning air carried the sweet scent of ripe cranberries and the distant rumble of traffic on I-40. Hawk took a deep breath, feeling the familiar tug of the rig as he settled into the routine of his daily commute.

Hawk lived in a small town just outside of Little Rock, Arkansas, where everyone knew each other's names and the local diner served up some of the best chicken fried steak this side of the Mississippi. He had grown up on these roads, learned to navigate them by heart, and had earned a reputation as one of the most reliable truckers in the region. But despite his tough exterior, Hawk had a soft spot for small towns and the people who called them home – especially if they were struggling like he was tonight.
