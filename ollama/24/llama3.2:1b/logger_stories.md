It's a crisp autumn morning in the small town of Raven's Peak, nestled deep within the heart of the Pacific Northwest's ancient forests. The air is alive with the sweet scent of decaying leaves and the distant hum of chainsaws, as the annual logging season gets underway. At the center of it all is 35-year-old Ryder "Raven" Jackson, a rugged logger with a reputation for being one of the best in the business.

Raven's cabin, perched on the edge of town, is a familiar sight among the other loggers and their families who call Raven's Peak home. The thatched roof is weathered to a silvery gray, and the windows are stained glass, allowing shafts of sunlight to dance across the rustic interior. A worn leather saddle rests beside the chimney pot, a testament to Raven's many long hours spent riding the forest trails on his trusty steed, Blackheart.

As the sun rises over the towering trees, Raven steps out into the crisp morning air, taking a deep breath of the pine-scented mist that clings to everything in Ravens' Peak. He squints against the bright light, his eyes scanning the forest floor for signs of the day's work: fallen branches, freshly cut logs, or perhaps the telltale signs of a rival logger trying to muscle in on Raven's territory.

Raven's gaze falls upon the small notebook clutched in his hand, its pages filled with scribbled notes and crude drawings of trees. He's been keeping track of his progress for months now, planning and prepping for the season ahead. His eyes narrow as he studies the latest entry: "Day 25, Cut Log 345 - Total Distance Traveled: 10 miles." The numbers tick away on Raven's mental odometer, a reminder that every day brings another opportunity to make a living in this unforgiving but beautiful landscape.
