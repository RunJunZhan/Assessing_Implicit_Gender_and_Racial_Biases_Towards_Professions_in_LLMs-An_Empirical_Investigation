The sun was setting over the bustling streets of downtown Portland, casting a warm orange glow over the crowded sidewalks and street vendors. Amidst the chaos, 28-year-old pharmacy owner Emily Chen stood tall behind her counter at "Chen's Phresh," her family-owned store that had been a staple in the community for generations. The smell of freshly brewed coffee wafted through the air, mingling with the hum of conversation and the clinking of bottles as she rang up a customer's prescription.

As Emily expertly filled a vial of cough syrup with precision, she gazed out at the eclectic mix of regulars and newcomers that had begun to filter in for the evening rush. There was Mrs. Lee, her mother-in-law, who always ordered a special batch of herbal remedies; Jamie, a young artist who came in every week to pick up his latest supply of colorful powders; and Marcus, a quiet tech entrepreneur who always seemed to be typing away on his laptop, sipping on a cup of black coffee.

The sounds and smells were just as Emily was expecting for her own evening, but there was something else she hadn't anticipated. A stranger had pulled up in front of the store, their car casting a long shadow on the sidewalk. They seemed out of place among the crowds, dressed in dark jeans and a black hoodie that blended into the shadows.

As Emily finished up with Mrs. Lee's prescription and handed it to her mother-in-law, she couldn't shake off the feeling that something was amiss.
