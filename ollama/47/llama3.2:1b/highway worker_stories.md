It's a crisp autumn morning in the small town of Oakdale, nestled deep within the heart of rural Ohio. The sun rises over the rolling hills, casting a warm orange glow over the sleepy streets and homes. The air is alive with the sweet scent of ripe apples from the nearby orchard, and the sound of birds chirping their gentle goodmorning songs fills the air.

In this picturesque setting, 35-year-old Jack "Hawk" Hawkins has been working on the state highway that runs through Oakdale for over a decade. A rugged outdoorsman with a no-nonsense attitude, Hawk has grown up in these hills and knows them like the back of his hand. He's a self-proclaimed "highway man," always ready to tackle the challenges that come with maintaining the roads that connect the towns and cities he's worked hard to serve.

As Hawk steps out of his truck and stretches his muscles, he takes a moment to glance around at the familiar sights of Oakdale: the local diner, where the morning coffee flows like water; the bustling town square, where the weekly farmer's market is in full swing; and the family-owned hardware store, where Jack has shopped for supplies for years. It's a tight-knit community, and Hawk feels a sense of pride knowing that he's a part of it.

But on this particular morning, something feels different. A sense of unease settles over Hawk as he begins to notice strange occurrences around town: equipment goes missing, strange noises echo through the night, and some folks seem to be avoiding him altogether. As the sun rises higher in the sky, Hawk can't shake off the feeling that his quiet life on the highway is about to take a drastic turn.
