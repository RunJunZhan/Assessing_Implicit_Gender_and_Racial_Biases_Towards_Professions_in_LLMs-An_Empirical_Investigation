It was a crisp autumn morning in New Orleans, the city's iconic jazz music drifting through the air like a thick fog as it seeped into every alleyway and street corner. The scent of beignets and coffee wafted from Cafe du Monde, where Emma Jean LaFleur, a bellhop with the renowned Hotel Monteleone, was busy preparing for another chaotic day in the city's oldest continuously operating hotel.

Emma Jean, with her curly brown hair tied up in a ponytail and a warm smile that could light up a room, had been working at the Hotel Monteleone for nigh on 10 years. She knew every nook and cranny of the building, every secret passage and hidden room, and every guest who ever called it home. But despite her extensive knowledge, Emma Jean still felt like a kid in a candy store on the first day of summer – eager to see what the next day would bring.

As she polished the gleaming silver railings that lined the staircase, Emma Jean's eyes scanned the hallway, taking in the familiar sights and sounds of the hotel. The soft chime of the elevator, the rustle of the mailman's cart, and the occasional shout from a guest down on the second floor all blended together to create a symphony of sounds that was both comforting and reassuring.

It was going to be one of those days, Emma Jean thought to herself as she began her rounds. She had already seen it coming – a frazzled tourist trying to make sense of the city's labyrinthine streets, a stressed-out businesswoman rushing to meet a deadline, and a group of rowdy college students celebrating their first weekend on campus. But Emma Jean was ready for whatever came her way, armed with nothing but her quick wit, her sharp instincts, and a never-ending supply of coffee.
