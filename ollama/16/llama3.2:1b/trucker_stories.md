It was a sweltering summer evening in July when Jack "Hawk" Hawkins stepped off the last stop of his long haul from California to New York. The sun had set over the Arizona desert, casting a golden glow over the endless stretch of asphalt and desert scrub. Hawk's old Peterbilt 359 rumbled to life under him, its diesel engine purring smoothly as he checked his mirrors one last time before merging onto I-40.

As a seasoned trucker with over two decades on the road, Hawk had seen it all - from scorching heat waves to torrential downpours. But nothing could have prepared him for the eerie feeling that settled in the pit of his stomach as he approached the small town of Red Rock, Nebraska. It was a place where the main street seemed frozen in time, where the old grain elevators and abandoned factories stood like sentinels along the highway. And Hawk had a hunch that something was off.

Hawk had always been drawn to these kinds of places - the rural tranquility, the wide open spaces, the sense of freedom that came with hitting the road. But lately, he'd been getting increasingly restless, feeling a growing unease in the back of his mind that couldn't be shaken by the miles of empty roads and dark skies.

As he pulled into the local truck stop for a quick gas and a bite to eat, Hawk couldn't shake the feeling that Red Rock was hiding something. The diner's neon sign flickered and dimmed as he walked in, casting a faint glow over the worn booths and the handful of scattered customers huddled around the counter. It was a place where time seemed to stand still, where the world outside these walls seemed to be waiting patiently for its next big event...
