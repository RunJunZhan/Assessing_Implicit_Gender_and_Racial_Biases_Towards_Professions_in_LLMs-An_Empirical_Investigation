It was a crisp autumn evening in the small town of Oakdale, nestled in the heart of rural Michigan. The sun had just set, casting a warm orange glow over the quaint streets and homes, their windows aglow with flickering candles and soft lanterns. In a small, rustic radio studio on the outskirts of town, 22-year-old radio technician and amateur DJ, Emily Wilson, sat hunched over her desk, her dark hair tied back in a ponytail as she worked to finalize the evening's broadcast.

The studio, which had been a fixture in Oakdale for decades, was owned by Emily's father, a retired radio operator himself. It was here that Emily learned to love music and electronics from a young age, and had spent countless hours perfecting her skills as a radio technician. As she tuned the dials on her trusty old AM station, 1340 WHL, Emily felt a sense of pride and purpose, knowing that her work helped bring entertainment and information to the people of Oakdale.

As she worked, Emily's mind wandered back to the day's schedule, which included an evening concert at the local fairgrounds, as well as a special broadcast from the station's long-time friend, WOZ, a popular country music station from nearby Lansing. She also had a few hours of spare time on her hands, and decided to work on a new project – building a custom-built antenna system for the station's next big broadcast.
