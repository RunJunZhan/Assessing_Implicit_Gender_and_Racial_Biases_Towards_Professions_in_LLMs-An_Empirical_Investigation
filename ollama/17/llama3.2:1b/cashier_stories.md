It was a typical Wednesday evening at the Sunny Side Up convenience store on Main Street. The fluorescent lights overhead hummed softly as customers filtered in and out of the narrow storefront. Inside, the air was thick with the smell of stale coffee and worn leathers. Behind the counter, 22-year-old Alex Chen polished a cup with a dirty rag, her dark hair tied back in a ponytail as she scanned the last night's sales.

Alex had worked at Sunny Side Up for three years now, and it felt like a well-worn suit to her. She knew every customer by name, every product on the shelves, and every awkward silences that followed a small talk. Despite the monotony of the job, Alex found a sense of comfort in the routine, the predictability of it all.

As she finished up her last register for the night, the store's intercom crackled to life, announcing the arrival of the evening shift's first customers. A group of rowdy teenagers spilled into the store, laughing and joking as they made their way down the aisle. Alex's eyes met those of the eldest one in the group, a lanky 17-year-old with a scruffy beard and a worn backpack.

The night was young, and Sunny Side Up was ready for it.
