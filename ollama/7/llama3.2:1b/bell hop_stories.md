It was a drizzly autumn evening in New Orleans, the kind of night that made you want to curl up with a warm cup of coffee and a good book on the French Quarter's charming streets. But for 22-year-old bell hop Colette Dupre, it was just another typical Friday night at the legendary Lafitte Guest House, a legendary hotel that had been welcoming weary travelers since 1795.

As she expertly balanced a tray of steaming hot coffee and sugar cubes on the counter of room 314, Colette couldn't help but feel a sense of pride and belonging. She had worked as a bell hop for Lafitte's for as long as she could remember, learning the ins and outs of the hotel from her father, who had taken over the job when Colette was just a little girl.

The room was a cozy haven, with plush velvet drapes and a comfortable four-poster bed that seemed to beckon anyone who stepped into it. Colette's coworker and friend, Jacques, was out on a delivery run down by the river, so she had the place all to herself. She took a moment to admire the intricate ironwork on the door handle, a family heirloom passed down from her father.

As she finished up the tray and began to wipe down the counter, Colette's mind wandered back to the phone call she had received earlier that day. Her boss at Lafitte was offering her a promotion, but it came with a catch: she would have to relocate to New York City for six months of training. Colette was torn – she loved this city and its energy, but she also missed the charm and magic of NOLA. She knew she had to make a decision soon, or risk losing the one job that made her feel truly alive.
