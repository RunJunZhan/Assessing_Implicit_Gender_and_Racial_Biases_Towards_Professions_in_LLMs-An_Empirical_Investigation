It's a crisp autumn morning in the small town of Oakdale, nestled deep within the heart of the Pacific Northwest's dense forest. The air is alive with the sweet scent of ripe huckleberries and the distant rumble of trucks carrying logs out to the nearby pulp mill. Among the forest's ancient trees, 35-year-old logger Ethan Thompson sits perched on a wooden stump at the edge of his family's logging company's property, watching as the sun rises over the treetops.

Ethan's worn denim jeans and scuffed up work boots are a testament to years of hard labor, but his weathered face still carries a hint of warmth from a lifetime spent outdoors. His dark hair is mussed, and a smudge of sap on his left cheekbone gives away his early morning coffee run the night before. He's a man who moves with the ease of a tree felled, his broad shoulders and calloused hands forged in the rugged beauty of the forest.

As he gazes out at the endless expanse of forest, Ethan feels a sense of contentment wash over him. This is what he was born for – the freedom of being outdoors, the satisfaction of bringing timber back to civilization, and the knowledge that his work helps keep the town's economy thriving. But despite the many joys of his life as a logger, Ethan can't shake the feeling that something is off. The forest has been changing over the years, and he's not sure if it's because of climate change or something else entirely – but he knows one thing for certain: he needs to get out here before it's too late.
