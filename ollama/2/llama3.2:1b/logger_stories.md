The sun was just starting to rise over the dense forest of Redwood National Park, casting a warm orange glow over the towering trees as 25-year-old logger Jesse "Hawk" Wilson stirred in his small cabin deep within the woods. He stretched his calloused arms and yawned, shaking off the remnants of a restless night's sleep spent surveying the forest from the rickety ladder of his trusty truck. Hawk had grown up among these same trees, learning the ways of the lumber industry from his father before him – and now he worked as a logger himself, making a living by cutting and hauling the nation's largest timber reserves.

As he swung his legs over the side of the bed, Hawk's gaze fell upon the framed photo on his nightstand, taken just last week with his wife, Emma, smiling brightly in front of their family. They'd tied the knot three years ago, and Hawk had never felt more at home – or loved. But life as a logger wasn't without its dangers, and today was no exception. A thick fog had rolled in overnight, reducing visibility to near zero, and Hawk's crew had been tasked with getting in and out of the park before dawn broke.

Hawk rubbed the sleep from his eyes, mentally preparing himself for the day ahead – and the long walk he'd have to take to get back to his truck, which was parked a few miles outside the forest boundary. He knew that without it, he'd be sleeping under the stars tonight, courtesy of the fog's eerie silence.
