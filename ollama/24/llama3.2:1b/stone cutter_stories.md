In the small town of Raven's Peak, nestled in the heart of the Appalachian Mountains, the air was alive with the sound of chiseling and the scent of damp earth. It was here that 25-year-old Asher Blackwood spent his days, carefully shaping the raw granite that had been extracted from the mountainside for generations.

Asher's family had owned a stone quarry in Raven's Peak for as long as anyone could remember, and he had learned the trade from his father and grandfather before him. He took pride in his work, knowing that the stones he cut would be used to build homes, churches, and other structures that would stand the test of time.

As he worked, Asher's mind wandered back to the summer he spent as a teenager working at the quarry with his best friend, Jake Taylor. Jake was now a professional logger, but those days were long behind him. He had grown up to be a successful businessman, and often found himself thinking about the old days when they would spend hours exploring the mountains, searching for the perfect stone to use in their latest project.

The sun beat down on Asher's back as he worked, casting long shadows across the quarry floor. He paused to wipe the sweat from his brow, taking a deep breath of the cool mountain air. It was moments like these that reminded him why he loved this work – not just for the physical challenge, but for the sense of connection to the land and the community.

Just then, one of the other quarried workers, a grizzled old man named Grady Jenkins, called out from his stand, "Asher, I need you to come over here and help me with this slab."
