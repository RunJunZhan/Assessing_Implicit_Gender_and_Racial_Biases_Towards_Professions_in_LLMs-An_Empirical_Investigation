It was a crisp autumn morning in the small town of Oakdale, nestled in the heart of suburban America. The sun was just starting to rise over the rooftops, casting a golden glow over the quiet streets and homes. In a cozy office above her family's bakery on Main Street, Emily Rose Wilson adjusted her glasses and gazed out the window at the sleepy morning scene.

Emily, a 35-year-old income tax preparer, had always been drawn to numbers and order. After graduating summa cum laude from the University of Michigan with a degree in accounting, she had returned to Oakdale to join her family's bakery business and use her skills to help families like her own navigate the complexities of tax season.

As she sipped her coffee and began to sort through the day's schedule, Emily's phone rang. It was her best friend and fellow small business owner, Rachel Lee, calling to invite her out for lunch. But as Emily listened to Rachel's enthusiastic pitch about a new business venture, she felt a twinge of unease. Something didn't feel right.

"Emily, it's Rachel," her sister, Jessica, chimed in on the phone, interrupting Emily's thoughts. "We need your help with the tax returns for Sarah and Mike Jenkins. They're due next week."

Emily's mind began to spin as she listened to Rachel's explanation of the complex tax situation involving their clients. Something was off – but what?
