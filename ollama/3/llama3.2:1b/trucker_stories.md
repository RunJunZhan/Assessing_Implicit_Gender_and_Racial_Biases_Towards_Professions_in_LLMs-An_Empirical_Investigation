It was a typical Monday morning in late spring as Jack "Hawk" Hawkins navigated his Peterbilt 389 truck through the darkening sky of rural Tennessee. The neon lights of the gas stations and diners along Highway 80 cast an eerie glow on the deserted roads, illuminating the faces of the few passengers Hawk had encountered overnight – a mix of sleepy truckers, late-night workers, and a lone young couple making their way to a wedding in Nashville.

Hawk's rig was emblazoned with his name and number on the side, a subtle reminder of who he was: a 20-year veteran of the road with over 1 million miles under his belt. His worn leather jacket, faded jeans, and scuffed boots told the story of a man who had spent countless hours on the open highway, from the mountains of West Virginia to the deserts of New Mexico.

As Hawk cruised down the highway, the wind whipping through his dark hair, he checked his watch for what felt like the hundredth time that morning. He was running late for a meeting with a potential new client at a truck stop in Knoxville, and he still had hours to go before he reached his destination. The rumble of engines and the occasional burst of laughter from fellow travelers broke up the silence of the highway, but Hawk's mind remained focused on the road ahead – and the looming mountain of paperwork that awaited him when he finally arrived.
