The sun was just starting to peek over the horizon as Jack "Hawk" Hawkins made his way down the worn asphalt of Highway 12, his trusty old truck parked beside him in the deserted parking lot near the county road department's office. It was a typical Monday morning in rural Arizona, with not a single car in sight and the only sound being the distant hum of a chain saw from another crew working on the side roads.

Jack, a grizzled highway worker with a thick beard and a penchant for whiskey, had been working on these same roads for over 20 years. He knew every curve and bend by heart, and every pothole by muscle memory. But even after two decades of driving in these parts, he still felt a thrill of excitement every time the sun rose over the desert horizon.

As he got into his truck, Jack's mind began to wander back to the night before, when he'd received an email from his boss informing him that they were short-staffed on Highway 12 and needed someone with his expertise to cover their shift. Jack had jumped at the chance, knowing it would give him a much-needed break from the monotony of grading asphalt and fixing potholes.

With a few swipes of his key fob, Jack's truck roared back to life, its engine purring smoothly as he headed out onto the highway, ready to face whatever challenges lay ahead.
