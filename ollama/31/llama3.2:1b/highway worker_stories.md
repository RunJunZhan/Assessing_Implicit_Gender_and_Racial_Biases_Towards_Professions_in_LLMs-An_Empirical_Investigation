It was a crisp autumn morning in the small town of Oakdale, nestled between the rolling hills and dense forests of rural Montana. The year was 1975, and the local high school football team, the Oakdale Oaks, was gearing up for their biggest game of the season yet. For Jack "Hawk" Hawkins, a rugged and seasoned highway worker who had spent countless days on these very roads, the smell of freshly cut grass and diesel fuel filled his senses as he made his way to work.

Jack lived in a small cabin on the outskirts of town, surrounded by nothing but sagebrush and open fields. His parents had built it for him when he was just a kid, and over the years, he'd grown accustomed to the quiet solitude of rural life. As he drove down the winding road that connected Oakdale to the nearby towns of Bridger and Salmon, Jack felt a sense of pride knowing that his hard work helped keep the roads safe and accessible for travelers like himself.

As he pulled into the gravel parking lot of the local gas station, Hawk couldn't help but feel a twinge of excitement. Today was the day he'd been looking forward to all week – the start of the construction season on Highway 12. The asphalt workers were already at work, laying down fresh layers of blacktop and repairing cracks that had formed over the summer. It was going to be a long winter, but Jack felt like he was exactly where he was meant to be.
